•Electricity: What is it?


Electricity is the flow of Electrons. It influences various phenomena such as electromagnetism and induction currents. Using these occurrences, it is possible to build machines and devices that serve almost any purpose.

  •To get started, there are two main ideas, that are critical for anyone devising circuits. Electricity at the right voltages and amperages can be deadly, so if you are just getting started it is best to stick to the predesigned circuits.

  •Formulas: Regarding electricity directly, is one core formula that runs the whole science. All others are based of this law(Ohms law), and must work around it.
V=(I)(R)

The V= Voltage, which is like the potential or water behind a dam.
I= measured in Amperes or Amps represents the amount of water flowing through the pipes(the rate of flow)
R= Resistance, measured in Ohms. This is like the putting your finger on the edge of a hose to cause the distance to increase.

  The other primary formula we will be using is
p=(I)(V)

It is useful for measuring battery lifetimes with theoretical circuit values for safety purposes.
P=Watts, V=Volts, and I=Current.


Ohms law, in Series.

The first type of circuit, is a series circuit. It has the advantage of series is that the resistance(r) and amperage(I) are incredibly easy to calculate. This is useful for providing high power systems for shorter times(on battery). It is commonly known that speed and power solve many things, that is why this type of circuit is commonly used for instruc tional purpouses. The formula for determining the resistance of a parallel circuit is as such:
R(t)=R1+R2+R3+R4...

This continues on for as long as the circuit does. Once this data is computed, the formula to solve for Amperes of consumption is
V=I*R, inputing R=R(t) and V= voltage tested. Once the data is calculated, wattage can be calculated and devised to determine how long the system will run on batteries, or if a power supply can "pump" enough amps to power the system.

  • Using a multimeter:
For anyone who wants to build or troubleshoot electronics, a multimeter is a must have tool. It allows for easy to measure voltages, resistance, and capacitance. Some even have more advanced settings such as frequency counters, temperature probes, and transistor testers.
$30 Cen-Tech Multimeter

To start, plug in the two test probes to the COM port, and the (V) port. To test if a battery is working, change the setting to V, and hold the test probes to the ends(+/- of the battery). Car batteries usually are between 12-14v DC, and AA batteries are around 1.5v. Do not try to measure amperage, as the battery will be drained instantly and the multimeter will need a new fuse. To test resistor values, change the meters setting to Ohms and connect both ends to the multimeter, and it will show the ohms. This is useful for checking connections later.


Ohms law, in Parallel.

The other type of circuit, is a parallel circuit. It has the advantage of keeping the same voltage the same while increasing the current draw(I). This is useful for maintaining a low power system for longer durations of time. The formula for determining the resistance of a parallel circuit is as such:
1/R(t)=1/R1+1/R2+1/R3...

This continues on for as long as the circuit does. Once this data is computed, the formula to solve for Amperes of consumption is
V=I*R, inputing R=R(t) and V= voltage tested. Once the data is calculated, wattage can be calculated and devised to determine how long the system will run on batteries, or if a power supply can "pump" enough amps to power the system.
